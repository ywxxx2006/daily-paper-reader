Abstract 

Content moderation in online multiplayer 3D virtual environments has recently been relegated to automated, AI-based pipelines. How-ever, the field has mainly been involved in detection of illicit content in images, video, and audio, leaving blind spots in detection tech-niques for suggestive motion. We present a motion-only classifica-tion pipeline that detects suggestive and explicit movement from SMPL skeleton trajectories using Laban Movement Analysis (LMA) descriptors. On 20,514 motion fragments (17+ hours) spanning four ordinal tiers—everyday, artistic, suggestive, explicit—logistic regres-sion over 110 LMA features achieves 57.3% four-way accuracy (2.3 ×

chance), 72.1% three-way, and 78.7% binary SFW/NSFW. Confusion concentrates on adjacent tiers, confirming that classification errors are concentrated between adjacent tiers over non-adjacent ones. Moreover, different movement qualities dominate at each level of the taxonomy — no single feature drives the classification, suggest-ing that the four-tier structure reflects genuinely distinct motion regimes.

1 Introduction 

3D social platforms such as VRChat, Roblox, and Rec Room allow users to animate avatars with arbitrary appearance. Conventional NSFW detection relies on pixel-level features; in practice, classifiers learn to flag revealing clothing, skin exposure, and body shape rather than the motion itself. We confirmed this directly: Qwen3-VL [Bai et al . 2025], a state-of-the-art multimodal LLM, cannot classify suggestive motion from skeleton-only visualizations. A moderation system for motion suggestiveness must therefore operate on motion rather than appearance. We observe that choreographic theory, specifically Rudolf La-ban’s Effort framework [Laban and Ullmann 1971], already provides a vocabulary for the qualitative shift from athletic to suggestive motion: safe movement is Bound, Strong, Sudden, Direct ; suggestive

We extract world-space 3D skeletons from video using WHAM [Shin et al . 2024] and compute LMA features following Turab et al. Using 55 Laban Movement Analysis descriptors extracted from SMPL skeleton trajectories, with no access to pixels, cloth-ing, or body shape, a logistic regression achieves 78.7% binary SFW/NSFW accuracy and 57.3% four-way ordinal accuracy across 20,514 motion fragments spanning 17 hours of video. Our contributions are: (1) We apply LMA descriptors [Turab et al . 2025], originally validated for dance style recognition, to the novel task of classifying motion along a suggestiveness gradient, using WHAM to jointly estimate floor contact and the 3D skeleton. (2) Empirical evidence that all four Laban Effort factors carry discriminative signal across binary (SFW/NSFW), three-way, and four-way classification, validating the LMA framework computationally. (3) Baseline classifiers across four-way, three-way, and binary formulations, establishing a lower bound for appearance-invariant content moderation from motion alone. 

2 Method 

Data. We construct a four-tier dataset of motion fragments: 

• Tier 0 (everyday: walking, sitting, dining) from Kinetics-700 [Kay et al. 2017] (19 classes, 1,075 fragments); 

• Tier 1 (artistic: breakdancing, ballet, capoeira, gymnastics) from Kinetics-700 (16 classes, 2,665 fragments); 

• Tier 2 (suggestive: twerk, dancehall, bachata sensual, heels, K-pop fancam) from TikTok/YouTube (7 categories, 2,172 fragments); 

• Tier 3 (explicit) from the NPDI academic corpus [Avila et al .2013] (14,602 fragments). All analysis uses balanced 1,075-per-class sampling. Total: 20,514 fragments from 1,788 videos spanning 17+ hours. 

Skeleton extraction. Each video passes through YOLO11x-pose filtering [Jocher et al . 2023] (retaining frames with ≥10 visible keypoints at confidence ≥0.5), continuous segment extraction ( ≥3 s), and WHAM inference [Shin et al . 2024] to produce per-frame SMPL joint positions in world space.

3 Results 

Accuracy. Logistic regression achieves 57.3% four-way accu-racy (F1 = 0.57, chance = 25%), 72.1% three-way (dropping Tier 1; chance = 33%), and 78.7% binary SFW/NSFW (chance = 50%). 

Ordinal confusion structure. The four-way confusion matrix (Fig-ure 2) reveals that the artistic–suggestive boundary (T1 ↔T2) is the hardest: 24% of T1 fragments are predicted as T2, and 20% of T2 as T1. Both involve continuous high-energy dance, but with different Effort qualities. The extremes recover cleanly: T0 = 66%, T3 = 64% recall. A counterintuitive finding is that T3 confuses more with T0 (20%) than with T2 (7%). 

Effort factor decomposition. Different Laban Effort qualities dom-inate at each classification granularity (Kruskal–Wallis 𝐻 ): Space 

(direct vs. indirect; 𝐻 =376 .7) is the top binary discriminator; Flow 

(bound vs. free; 𝐻 =608 .6) rises to rank 4 in three-way; Time (sudden vs. sustained; 𝐻 =826 .8) reaches rank 3 in four-way. All four Effort pillars carry signal somewhere in the taxonomy—a computational validation of the LMA framework. 

The Directness Paradox. In the binary setting, six of the top ten discriminative features measure joint Directness . Everyday motion is high-Directness: the body goes somewhere. Suggestive motion is low-Directness: joints trace recirculating, indirect paths that 

display rather than navigate . This confirms Laban’s prediction that the functional-to-suggestive shift is carried by the Direct →Indirect polarity of the Space factor [Hanna [n. d.]; Laban and Ullmann 1971]. 

4 Discussion 

Limitations. Each tier is sourced from a different domain (Ki-netics, TikTok/YouTube, NPDI), introducing potential confounds from camera angle and WHAM reconstruction quality. A domain-adversarial ablation is planned to disentangle motion signal from source artifacts. The current 110-dim summary discards temporal dynamics; a sequence model over the raw (𝑇 × 55 ) features is a nat-ural extension that should particularly improve T1/T2 separation. 

Future work. The 57% four-way baseline establishes the floor that temporally-attentive models must beat. We plan to (1) train an ordinal multiple-instance learning (OMIL) classifier [Ruiz et al .2016] on noisily-labeled web-scraped data at scale, specifically tar-geting the T1/T2 boundary; (2) apply domain-adversarial training [Ganin et al . 2016] to confirm that the signal is motion-intrinsic; and (3) evaluate generalization to rendered avatar motion in 3D virtual environments. Concretely, we plan to fine-tune Motion-BERT [Zhu et al . 2023], a skeleton foundation model pretrained on Appearance-Invariant Detection of Suggestive Motion via Laban Movement Descriptors on SMPL Skeletons SIGGRAPH Posters ’26, July 19–23, 2026, Los Angeles, CA, USA 

large-scale motion data. A two-stream architecture combining Mo-tionBERT’s learned temporal representations with the interpretable LMA descriptors presented here would preserve the auditability required for detection of inappropriate content while capturing motion dynamics that per-fragment summary statistics discard. 

Implications. That a logistic regression on LMA features achieves 79% binary accuracy from skeleton alone—with no appearance information—shows that motion suggestiveness is detectable from skeletal motion data. The interpretability of LMA features makes the system auditable: each feature has a name, a choreographic meaning, and a century of dance-theory context [Chi et al . 2000; Samadani et al. 2013].
